# The Scenario — The Universal "50/50" Moment

> This is the human moment ConceptLoop is built around. It doubles as the core marketing
> narrative: nearly every student who has ever sat a multiple-choice exam has lived it.

## The moment everyone recognizes

You're working through a practice question. You read the stem, and one of two things happens:

1. **You already know the answer.** You barely need the options — you have the answer in your
   head and you're just scanning for the one that matches. (More on this case below.)
2. **You have a rough idea.** You start reading the options and immediately begin *crossing
   off* the ones you know are wrong.

Either way, the dangerous case is the same one: you cross off the obviously-wrong options
and you're left **stuck between two**. The wording is tricky, or there's a genuine gap in
your understanding. You go back and forth. Eventually you go with a **gut feeling**.

Then you get it right — or wrong — and in that 50/50 moment, **luck did a lot of the work.**

## Why this moment matters

That final two-option deliberation is the single most information-rich instant in studying,
and every existing tool throws the information away:

- A flashcard app records "wrong" and shows you the same card later.
- A quiz bank records "wrong" and gives you an explanation you skim once.
- Neither knows *which two options* you were torn between, or *why*.

But the two options you couldn't separate are a precise fingerprint of your misconception.
If you were stuck between potassium and calcium, your confusion is specific and nameable. If
you'd crossed off the *correct* answer entirely and were debating two wrong ones, that's a
different and deeper gap. Same "wrong," completely different problem.

## What ConceptLoop does with the moment

When you miss a question, ConceptLoop doesn't just say "wrong, here's why." It looks at:

- the question stem,
- the options you crossed off (what you were confident *wasn't* the answer),
- the two you were stuck between,
- the answer you chose, and
- the **verified correct answer** (which you supplied),

and it infers the *specific* reason you were stuck. Then, later, it re-presents the same
underlying concept through a **different question** — a new angle, more context, or simpler
framing — so you're tested on whether you actually understand the concept, not whether you
remember the original wording.

The 50/50 stops being a coin flip. The thing you got lucky (or unlucky) on comes back in a
form that forces you to actually know it.

## Two flavors of the miss (both matter)

| Situation | What it reveals | What ConceptLoop should do |
|---|---|---|
| **Correct answer survived the cross-off, but you picked the other one** | A *discrimination* failure — you understand the area but can't separate two close concepts, or the wording tricked you. | Sharpen the boundary between those two specific concepts; re-test from a new angle. |
| **You crossed off the correct answer and deliberated between two wrong ones** | A *deeper gap* — the right concept wasn't even in your consideration set. | Re-teach the concept first, *then* re-test. More serious than case 1. |

The cross-off data is the only reason we can tell these two apart. That's why the cross-off
interaction is central, not cosmetic.
